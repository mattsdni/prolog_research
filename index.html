A high-level computer programming language first devised for artificial intelligence applications.

Development

Conceived in the early 1970s by Alain Colmerauer and Phillipe Roussel at the University of Aix-Marseille.

Developed in 1972 in collaboration with Robert Kowalski from the University of Edinburgh

Kowalski contributed the theoretical framework while Colmerauer's research provided means to formalize the Prolog language

ISO Prolog standardization in 1995


Influenced by PLANNER - Designed by Carl Hewitt at MIT in 1969

Influenced Visual Prolog, Mercury, Oz, Erlang, Strand, KL0, KL1, Datalog, CHR, XSB

Features

Program logic is expressed in terms of relations, and a computation is initiated by running a query over these relations. Relations are defined by clauses. Prolog's single data type is the term. Terms are either atoms, numbers, variables or compound terms.


An example of a fact is:

cat(tom).

which is equivalent to the rule:

cat(tom) :- true.

Given the above fact, one can ask:

is tom a cat?

?- cat(tom).
Yes

what things are cats?

?- cat(X).
X = tom

Clauses with bodies are called rules. An example of a rule is:

animal(X) :- cat(X).

If we add that rule and ask what things are animals?

?- animal(X).
X = tom

Uses

Prolog is a general purpose logic programming language associated with artificial intelligence and computational linguistics. Also good for database, symbolic mathematics, and language parsing.

Medical research

Online support services for customers

Build expert systems that solve complex problems without the help of humans (automatically planning, monitoring, controlling and troubleshooting complex systems

Build decision support systems that aid organizations in decision-making
